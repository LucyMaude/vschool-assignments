About:

Haiku is a form of traditional Japanese verse. It usually consists of a three-line poem with seventeen "on" (typically translated to syllables), written in a 5 / 7 / 5 count. Often focusing on images from nature, haiku emphasizes simplicity and directness of expression.

Frequently the directness of haiku is exemplified by the inclusion of a "kireji" or "cutting word". The kireji finds its' home between two contrasting images.

Haiku began in thirteenth-century Japan as the opening phrase of renga, an oral poem, which was also composed syllabically. The much shorter haiku broke away from renga in the sixteenth-century.

Anatomy

The first stanza should be 5 syllables

An old silent pond...

The second should be 7

A frog jumps into the pond,

The last, 5 syllables again

splash! Silence again.

Notice the use of "splash" between the image of a from jumping and the stillness of the pond. (kireji)

by Basho (1644-1694)
